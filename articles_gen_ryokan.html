To Our Luxembourg Readers

Aomori is not Tokyo. It is the northern frontier, a land of deep snow and deeper resilience. For the European traveler who has seen the neon lights of Shinjuku and the temples of Kyoto, 'KAI Tsugaru' offers something rarer: total, white silence.

Frozen Time: The Snowy Solitude of KAI Tsugaru

Immerse yourself in 'Kamakura' (snow huts) and ancient hot springs. A luxury ryokan where winter is not endured, but celebrated.

1. The Sound of Snow: Tsugaru Shamisen

The snow in Tsugaru is different. It is heavy, wet, and relentless. To silence the howling wind, the locals developed the 'Tsugaru Shamisen'. Unlike the delicate plucking seen in Kyoto geisha districts, the Tsugaru style is percussive, aggressive, and soulful. It is the jazz of the north.

Every evening, KAI Tsugaru hosts a live performance in a hall decorated with 'Buna' (Japanese Beech) wood art. Guests sit captivated as the three strings mimic the blizzard outside. It is a powerful reminder that beauty often comes from hardship.

🇯🇵 Keyword: Yukimi-Buro (雪見風呂)

Literally "Snow-Viewing Bath". It captures the quintessential Japanese contrast: the biting cold air on your face versus the volcanic heat of the water wrapping your body. Floating a sake cup in the water while watching snowflakes dissolve into the steam is considered the ultimate winter luxury.

2. Oma Tuna: The Black Diamond

Just north of Tsugaru lays the Tsugaru Strait, separating the main island from Hokkaido. In these treacherous currents swims the 'Oma Tuna', known as the Black Diamond of the sea. During winter, these fish build up incredible layers of fat to survive the cold.

At KAI Tsugaru, the Kaiseki dinner features this tuna in a tasting course called 'Maguro Zukushi'. You will experience different cuts—Akami (lean), Chutoro (medium fatty), and Otoro (fatty)—prepared in ways that highlight their distinct textures. It is kilometer-zero dining at its most exclusive.

3. Tsugaru Lacquerware: The Colors of Wait

Throughout the ryokan, you will notice intricate, speckled patterns on tables, chopsticks, and wall art. This is 'Tsugaru Nuri'. To achieve this pattern, craftsmen apply 48 layers of lacquer over several months, polishing them down to reveal the colorful layers beneath. It is said to represent the endurance of the northern people waiting for spring.
